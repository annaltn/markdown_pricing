Color popularity¶

This measures how much a color is popular among customers. It is natural that popularity of colors will vary with category (sub-cat in our case). For example, we expect that for kids apparel, joyful colors like red, pink, yellow are preferred; while for shirts, colors like white, blue are preferred. Thus, we should compute color popularity for each sub-cat. Moreover, we expect that color popularity will NOT change with time (it may change from year to year but not within one year). In short, color popularity only depends on color and sub-cat.

Def¶

Popularity of a color $c$ under a sub-cat $k$ is the following ratio: $$ pop(c, k) = \dfrac{n(c, k)}{\sum_c^\prime n(c^\prime, k)} $$ where $n(c, k)$ is the number of sold units which are under sub-cat $k$ and of color $c$.

Size popularity¶

Defs¶

Popularity of a size¶

Given size $s$, its popularity under sub-cat $k$ is: $$ pop(s, k) = \dfrac{m(s, k)}{\sum_s^\prime m(s^\prime, k)} $$ where $m(s, k)$ is the number of sold units which are under sub-cat $k$ and of size $s$.

Size popularity of a config¶

Given a config $cf$ under sub-cat $k$, its size popularity will be the weighted average of its sizes' popularity: $$ pop_{size}(cf, k) = \sum_{s \in S_{cf}} w_s * pop(s, k) $$

where

  • $S_{cf}$ is the set of sizes of the config

  • $ w_s = \frac{n(cf, s)}{\sum_{s^\prime \in S_{cf}} n(cf, s^\prime)} $ is the weight of size $s$

A challenge when working with sizes is that they are not normalized yet. We have all kinds of sizes, as shown below. Thus, we first need a mapping to normalize sizes.

Given a config $cf$ and a date $d$, its sell through rate $ r(cf, d) $ is defined as the following ratio: $$ r(cf, d) = \dfrac{n(cf, d)}{s_{cf, 0}} $$ where $n(cf, d)$ is the no. of $cf$'s units sold up to date $d$ and $s_{cf, 0}$ is initial stock of $cf$. Both $n(cf, d)$ and $s_{cf, 0}$ should be summed over all simples of the config.

Competition-based features¶

No. of competitors¶

Given a config, its competitors are defined as other configs in the same group (determined by sub-categ, gender, season) which are visible. The number of competitors will change over time when configs entering or exiting from a group (i.e. becomes visible/invisible). Thus, for each visible config $cf$ in group $g$, we define its no. of competitors on date $d$ as: $$ n_c(cf, d) = n_c := n_{viz}(g, d) - 1 $$ where $n_{viz}(g, d)$ is the number of visible configs of group $g$ on date $d$.

Relative price against competitors¶

Given config $cf$ in group $G$, we define $cf$'s relative price on date $d$ against competing configs as the ratio between $cf$'s price and sum of prices of configs in $G$. $$ rp(cf, G, d) = \dfrac{p(cf, d)}{\sum_{cf^\prime \in G} p(cf^\prime, d)} $$

  • Get the slice of visible configs of group on date $d$ and calculate relative prices for those configs:
In [5]:
def get_slice(group_df, d):
    cols = ['sku_config', 'snapshot_date', 'price', 'current_price', 'special_price', 'cost_supplier']
    sub_df = group_df.query('snapshot_date == "{}" and is_visible == 1'.format(d))[cols]
    sub_df = sub_df.drop_duplicates() # dups due to simples of the same config
    return sub_df

Time features¶

These include:

  • Time to end of season (EoS)
  • in_season: an indicator for whether a product is still in its 'correct' season
  • is_weekend: indicates whether a date is weekend or weekday
  • is_holiday: indicates whether a date is a holiday
  • in_grand_sale: indicates whether a date is within grand-sale season (specific to SG)

These features are currently not available yet. The first two are assigned to Alyona but waiting for hard-coded EoS date (from buying team).
